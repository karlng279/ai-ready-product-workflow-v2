# Product Strategy — Rules

## What Product Strategy Is

Product strategy answers three questions: Where are we going? Why this direction? How do we win? It is not a feature roadmap — it is the framework that makes roadmap decisions obvious.

A strong strategy is a coherent set of choices about what you will do AND what you will NOT do. Without explicit trade-offs, you have a wish list, not a strategy.

---

## Required Components

Every product strategy artifact must include all five layers:

### 1. Vision & Mission
- **Vision:** What the world looks like when you succeed. Long-horizon (5–10 years), aspirational.
- **Mission:** Why the company exists and who it serves. Enduring, not tied to a specific product.
- Format: One sentence each. No corporate jargon. A new employee should understand it in 10 seconds.

### 2. Strategic Position
- Where do you play? (market, segment, buyer, use case)
- How do you win? (differentiation, cost leadership, niche focus)
- What makes this defensible over time? (network effects, switching costs, data, brand)
- Use the **Positioning Statement** format: "For [target customer] who [has this need], [product name] is a [category] that [key benefit]. Unlike [alternatives], [product name] [differentiator]."

### 3. OKR Hierarchy
- **Objective:** Qualitative, inspiring direction. 1–3 per quarter per team.
- **Key Results:** 2–5 measurable outcomes per Objective. Must be numeric, time-bound.
- **Initiatives:** Specific bets the team is making to achieve Key Results. Prioritized, not exhaustive.
- Rule: KRs measure outcomes, not outputs. "Ship feature X" is not a KR. "Increase activation rate from 40% to 60%" is.

### 4. Competitive Analysis
- Direct competitors: same product, same buyer, same use case.
- Indirect competitors: different product, same buyer, alternative behaviour.
- Substitutes: no product at all (spreadsheets, email, paper).
- For each: assess strengths, weaknesses, pricing, go-to-market motion.
- Conclusion: where do we win, where do we lose, and why?

### 5. Strategic Bets
- 3–5 explicit choices the team is making.
- Each bet: assumption behind it, evidence for it, what would prove it wrong.
- These are the bets you are NOT making — list at least 2 strategic non-bets.

## Frameworks to Apply

### Product Strategy Canvas (Strategyzer)
Use for: framing the overall strategy on one page.
Sections: Vision, Target Customer, Value Proposition, Key Activities, Key Resources, Competitive Moat, Revenue Model.

### OKR Hierarchy
Use for: connecting strategy to quarterly execution.
Structure: Company OKRs → Product OKRs → Team OKRs → Individual contributions.

### Porter's Five Forces
Use for: assessing industry attractiveness and competitive dynamics.
Forces: Threat of new entrants, Bargaining power of suppliers, Bargaining power of buyers, Threat of substitutes, Competitive rivalry.

### Ansoff Matrix
Use for: growth strategy selection.
Quadrants: Market Penetration (existing product + existing market), Product Development (new product + existing market), Market Development (existing product + new market), Diversification (new product + new market).

### Jobs-to-be-Done (Strategic lens)
Use for: reframing competition and identifying non-obvious threats.
Core idea: customers hire products to get jobs done. The real competitor is whoever else gets the job done — including doing nothing.

---

## Quality Checklist

Before finalizing a strategy artifact:
- [ ] Vision and mission are each one clear, jargon-free sentence
- [ ] Positioning statement uses the correct format and names a specific alternative
- [ ] Each OKR Key Result is numeric and time-bound
- [ ] Competitive analysis covers direct, indirect, and substitutes
- [ ] Strategic bets are explicit — and strategic non-bets are listed
- [ ] Strategy addresses the "how do we win" question, not just "what we will build"

---

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- **Strategy as roadmap:** Listing features is not a strategy. A strategy explains WHY those features, in what order, against which competitors, for which customers.
- **Vague positioning:** "The best platform for everyone" is not a position. A position requires trade-offs.
- **KRs as outputs:** "Launch 5 features" is an output. "Increase paid conversion from 3% to 8%" is an outcome.
- **Ignoring non-customers:** The most important strategic insight often comes from understanding why people DON'T buy from you.
