# Go-to-Market — Rules

## What GTM Is

Go-to-market answers: How do we get the product into the hands of the right customers, at the right time, through the right channels? A GTM strategy is not a launch checklist — it is a coherent set of decisions about who to sell to first, how to reach them, what to charge, and how to enable Sales and Marketing to win.

---

## Required Components

### 1. Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)
The ICP defines the customer most likely to buy quickly, use deeply, and expand over time.

**B2B ICP dimensions:**
- Firmographic: Industry, company size (employees, revenue), geography, tech stack
- Behavioral: Buying process, decision-makers, evaluation criteria
- Situational: Triggering event that makes them ready to buy now (e.g., headcount growth, compliance deadline, new CTO)
- Success profile: What does a successful customer look like 12 months post-sale?

**B2C ICP dimensions:**
- Behavioral: Usage patterns, purchase triggers, referral behavior
- Psychographic: Values, goals, identity
- Situational: Life event or context that creates the job-to-be-done

Rule: The ICP is not "everyone who could theoretically buy." It is the customers in the top 20% who generate 80% of value. Be ruthlessly specific.

### 2. Beachhead Market
Identify the smallest viable segment you can dominate first (Geoffrey Moore, Crossing the Chasm):
- Must be small enough to win decisively
- Must be large enough to sustain the business in the near term
- Must be adjacent to other segments you want to enter next
- Winning criterion: >50% market share in the beachhead within 18–24 months

### 3. GTM Motion
Choose the primary motion that matches your product and ICP:

| Motion | Best for | Signal |
|--------|----------|--------|
| **Product-Led Growth (PLG)** | Self-serve, low ACV, viral potential | Users discover value without a salesperson |
| **Sales-Led Growth (SLG)** | High ACV, complex buying committee, long sales cycle | Needs a champion, demo, and procurement |
| **Marketing-Led Growth (MLG)** | Content-heavy, thought leadership, community | Buyers educate themselves before contacting Sales |
| **Community-Led Growth** | Developer tools, creator platforms, niche communities | Network effects from community participation |
| **Hybrid (PLG + SLG)** | Product as top-of-funnel, Sales for expansion | Freemium drives adoption, Sales closes enterprise |

### 4. Launch Plan
Three-phase structure:
- **Alpha (closed beta):** 10–20 design partners. Goal: validate core value with high-trust customers. No marketing.
- **Beta (open beta):** 100–500 users. Goal: validate activation and retention. Light marketing.
- **GA (General Availability):** Full launch. Goal: scale acquisition. Full marketing playbook.

Each phase: entry criteria, success metrics, exit criteria (what must be true to move to next phase).

### 5. Pricing Strategy
Choose pricing model based on value delivered and competitive context:

| Model | Use when | Example |
|-------|----------|---------|
| **Value-based** | Strong ROI case; customers measure outcome | "Saves 10 hours/week" → price as % of value |
| **Competitive** | Undifferentiated market; price sensitivity high | Match or undercut leader |
| **Cost-plus** | Commodity; tight margins | Cost × (1 + margin %) |
| **Freemium** | High volume, low ACV; viral potential | Free tier drives acquisition; paid converts power users |
| **Usage-based** | Value scales with usage | API calls, seats, GB stored |

Rule: Test pricing before launch. Survey willingness-to-pay using Van Westendorp or Conjoint Analysis.

### 6. Battlecard
One battlecard per top competitor. Structure:
- **Our strengths vs. this competitor:** 3–5 points where we win
- **Their strengths vs. us:** Be honest — what will prospects throw at you?
- **Landmines to plant:** Questions to ask prospects that expose competitor weaknesses
- **Trap questions they set:** Questions competitors ask that hurt us — and how to redirect
- **Win stories:** 2–3 customer quotes or case studies from head-to-head wins

## Frameworks to Apply

### ICP Definition (B2B)
Firmographic + Behavioral + Situational attributes. Validated against closed-won data.

### Beachhead Market (Geoffrey Moore)
Crossing the Chasm: win the bowling pin closest to you before targeting adjacent pins.

### GTM Motion Selection
Choose motion by product type + ACV + buyer complexity. Document reasoning.

### Launch Plan (Alpha → Beta → GA)
Each phase has entry criteria, metrics, and exit criteria. Not a date-driven calendar.

### Van Westendorp Pricing Model
Four survey questions: Too cheap / Cheap / Expensive / Too expensive → find acceptable price range.

### Battlecard Template
One per top competitor. Updated quarterly or after major competitive events.

---

## Quality Checklist

Before finalizing a GTM artifact:
- [ ] ICP includes firmographic, behavioral, AND situational attributes
- [ ] Beachhead market is specific enough to achieve >50% share
- [ ] GTM motion explicitly chosen with reasoning; hybrid motion explained
- [ ] Launch plan has 3 phases with entry/exit criteria (not just dates)
- [ ] Pricing tested against willingness-to-pay data
- [ ] Battlecard exists for each top-3 competitor

---

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- **ICP as "everyone":** No trade-offs = no ICP. "Mid-market companies" is not an ICP.
- **Launch as a single event:** Launches are phases, not moments. Big-bang launch without alpha/beta learning is risky.
- **Pricing as an afterthought:** Price communicates positioning. Setting it last means you've built a product without knowing if your economics work.
- **Battlecards that only list your strengths:** Battlecards must include honest assessment of competitor strengths — otherwise Sales gets caught flat-footed.
- **GTM motion mismatch:** Using PLG for a product that requires implementation + training, or SLG for a $50/month self-serve product, both fail.
