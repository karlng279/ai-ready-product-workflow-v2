# Market Research — Rules

## What Market Research Is

Market research answers: Who are our customers? How large is the opportunity? What does the customer journey look like? It provides the factual foundation for product decisions — without it, personas are stereotypes and market sizing is guesswork.

Good market research is specific, segmented, and behavioral. It distinguishes between what customers say they want and what they actually do.

---

## Required Components

### 1. Customer Segmentation
Before writing a persona, define your segmentation model:
- **Behavioral segmentation:** Grouped by how they behave (usage frequency, jobs hired for, switching triggers)
- **Psychographic segmentation:** Grouped by values, goals, fears
- **Firmographic segmentation (B2B):** Company size, industry, tech stack, buying process
- **Demographic segmentation:** Only useful as a secondary attribute, never primary

Rule: B2C products segment by behavior and psychographics. B2B products segment by firmographic first, then by individual role.

### 2. Persona Creation
Each persona must include:
- **Name + role:** Fictional but specific. Not "Operations Manager" but "Sarah, Senior Operations Manager at a mid-size freight forwarder, 8 years in role."
- **Functional job:** The main task they hire a product to do
- **Emotional job:** How they want to feel when the job is done
- **Social job:** How they want to be perceived
- **Current solution:** What they use today (and why it's inadequate)
- **Pains:** Frustrations with the current approach
- **Gains:** What success looks like for them
- **Switching trigger:** What would make them change solutions

Rule: Personas are grounded in real interviews, not assumptions. Each claim should trace back to a customer quote or behavioral observation.

### 3. Market Sizing (TAM / SAM / SOM)
- **TAM (Total Addressable Market):** Everyone who could theoretically buy this category of product. Top-down from industry reports.
- **SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market):** The segment you can actually reach with your current model. Bottom-up from real customer data.
- **SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market):** Realistic capture rate in 3–5 years given your GTM, team, and competitive position.

Rule: Always calculate bottom-up AND top-down. If they differ by more than 3x, explain why.

Bottom-up formula: (Number of target companies or users) × (% willing to pay) × (Average contract value or ARPU)

### 4. Customer Journey Map
Map the full experience from first awareness to advocacy:
- **Stages:** Awareness → Consideration → Decision → Onboarding → Active Use → Expansion → Advocacy
- For each stage: Touchpoints, Customer actions, Customer emotions (use + / - notation), Company actions, Opportunities to improve
- Identify: Moments of delight, Moments of friction, Drop-off points

Rule: Journey maps are cross-functional artifacts — include Sales, Marketing, Support, and Product touchpoints.

## Frameworks to Apply

### Jobs-Aware Personas
Combines traditional persona attributes (demographics, context) with JTBD (functional/emotional/social jobs, switching trigger). More actionable than demographic personas because it explains the WHY behind customer behavior.

### TAM / SAM / SOM
Standard market sizing approach. Always do both top-down (industry reports → your segment share) and bottom-up (number of buyers × price) and reconcile.

### Customer Journey Map (CJM)
Full-lifecycle view of the customer experience. Used to identify drop-off points, emotional friction, and growth opportunities across the funnel.

### Blue Ocean Strategy (Kim & Mauborgne)
Use for: identifying uncontested market space.
Tools: Strategy Canvas (plot features vs. competitors), Four Actions Framework (Eliminate, Reduce, Raise, Create).

---

## Quality Checklist

Before finalizing market research:
- [ ] Segmentation model is behavioral, not just demographic
- [ ] Each persona traces claims to real interviews or behavioral data
- [ ] TAM calculated top-down AND bottom-up; discrepancy explained
- [ ] SOM includes realistic assumptions about GTM, team capacity, and competitive position
- [ ] Journey map covers all stages from Awareness to Advocacy
- [ ] Competitive analysis includes substitutes (non-product alternatives)

---

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- **Demographic personas:** "35-year-old male in the US" tells you nothing about product decisions. Segment by behavior.
- **Top-down only market sizing:** "The logistics market is $500B, we need 1% = $5B" is a fiction. Do the bottoms-up.
- **Journey maps without emotions:** A journey map that only shows steps (not feelings) misses where the real friction is.
- **Competitor analysis without substitutes:** The biggest competitive threat is often "do nothing" or "use a spreadsheet," not another software product.
